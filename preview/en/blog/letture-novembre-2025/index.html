The book, like the spoon, belongs to those objects that are invented once and for all

November 2025

Come ordinare una biblioteca, by Roberto Calasso

The book, like the spoon, belongs to those objects that are invented once and for all — in ancient times or even quite recently. Our repertoire of gestures is extremely limited. And objects are more or less successful attempts to adapt to the inevitable characteristics of these gestures.

Monsieur Teste, by Paul Valéry

A dense book, laden with meaning. Fragmented, it comes from writings and letters and notes from different times and contexts. This character, Monsieur Teste, is a pataphysical opposite: he masters reason, while material action remains alien to him. Toward the end, the demon's question: he demonstrates that he is still the same person you believed him to be.

The description of the train journey that inevitably leads to modern literature is wonderful.

Il culto dei morti, by Henry James

A novel of incredible intimate topicality and social forgetting. The way we relate to the death of loved ones. The book loses itself in a series of forced coincidences, which undermine the claim to realism necessary to literature. It's highly commendable that in such a short book, decades are recounted. Not a speech, not a dialogue, only description of the protagonist's inner state. Hints at his life help us understand that he is wealthy, but nothing more. A non-character who acts and interacts with his dead and his living. Even his past is merely hinted at. What remains crucial, beyond the relationship of the living with their dead, is the relationship of the individual with others. The protagonist designs with maniacal care an interior temple. In this way he makes it evident, objective, real. Thus it can be internalized by others, giving rise to misunderstanding between him and the woman with a single dead.

Il cacciatore celeste, by Roberto Calasso

A dense book, metamorphic, mystical. Myth offers two contradictory realities: one must find a way to accept both. Every hero must kill a monster, every hero must become that monster.

Simulation, imitation, possession, metamorphosis: the rose of the winds of the psyche. And a serpent that bites its tail. They are kindred powers: sometimes they amalgamate, sometimes they oppose, sometimes they touch. Where they act, it's easy to be confused. The art of distinguishing them was called discretion of spirits.

I reflect... And is it something very different from that practice that consisted (and still consists) in consulting the "spirits"? Waiting in front of a table, a deck of cards, an idol or a dormient and moaning Pythia, or instead in front of what is called "ourselves"...
